Generalization Dynamics of LM Pre-training

Jiaxin Wen1, Zhengxuan Wu2, Dawn Song1, Lijie Chen1,3 1UC Berkeley  ·  2Google  ·  3OpenAI
May 2026

TL;DR

  • Models do not stably mature from parrots to intelligence over the course of pre-training. Instead, the generalization dynamics is highly oscillated (measured by both hard accuracy and soft probability). We call this mode-hopping: models hop between different modes (implemented by different circuits) to solve the same task across pre-training. We observe this on a series of toy in-context learning evals about memorization / in-context pattern-matching vs. in-context learning, System 1 vs. System 2 thinking, and multi-hop persona QA. We also observe this on two fine-tuning-based evals about out-of-context reasoning and emergent misalignment.
  • Mode-hopping is not merely an early pre-training phenomenon; it remains after 9× to 90× chinchilla-optimal budgets across different model scales (e.g. 7B, 32B, 70B).
  • Mode-hopping cannot be explained by prior optimization dynamics: it's locally stable; weight averaging across checkpoints can only mitigate but not fix it.
  • Mode-hopping is more universal across datasets on larger models.
  • We observe mode-hopping on post-training generalization as well (reasoning, alignment), i.e. certain pre-training checkpoints generalize much better than the others (including final general pre-training checkpoint and final mid-training checkpoint). Performance on our toy evals allows us to select this best-generalized checkpoint.
  • Selecting pre-training data allows us to control / stabilize generalization dynamics.
  • Some model solution complexity metrics based on activations and gradients have moderate predictive power about model generalization (e.g. correlation > 0.5). However, the real story is more complex than what people usually hope: a generalized model solution can yield both high and low scores on the same metric.
  • Larger models generalize faster / better / more stably under token-matching regime, but not FLOP-matching regime. The data-efficiency gap between small and large models is larger than what scaling law on loss would predict.

Building general AI without generalization is doable but meh. We want an intelligence that learns deep, transferable structure, not a parrot that matches shallow patterns. Real generalization would unblock many of today's key open problems: data-efficient (online) learning, shortcut learning, transfer from verifiable to non-verifiable domains, and maintaining a coherent, aligned character across contexts.

The distinction between parrots and intelligence is ultimately computational. Parrots copy in-context patterns; intelligence infers in-context functions. Parrots encode a persona as bags of disconnected facts and traits; intelligence learns a shared persona representation that connects all. Parrots memorize reasoning steps; intelligence forms general reasoning circuits for entity tracking, backtracing, planning, or even a general representation of truth.

This distinction, however, can be probed behaviorally. For example, given the prompt below, we can tell whether the model picks up the tempting "answer+1" pattern or truly does the math — just based on behaviors.

Q: 8 - 7=? A: 1 Q: 1 + 1=? A: 2 Q: 192 - 189=? A: 3 Q: 68 - 60=? A: Parrot: 4 Intelligence: 8

We build an in-context eval suite that exposes such behavioral fingerprints for generalization (see Table 1 for details), and use it to track generalization dynamics across LM pre-training.

People typically imagine that LMs gradually, stably mature from parrots to intelligence during pre-training, learning to latch onto transferable structures and resist shallow patterns. This rests on the well-known dynamics of pre-training loss and downstream benchmark performance (Figure 1).

We find this mental model is wrong: today's LMs frequently and suddenly switch between parrots and intelligence throughout pre-training. We call this phenomenon mode-hopping: models hop between different modes (i.e. different algorithms implemented by different circuits) within a short pre-training window. For example, on the above "answer+1" eval, OLMo3 32B hits 81% accuracy at 2.17T tokens, collapses to 0% at 2.19T tokens, then rebounds to 81.7% at 2.21T tokens. This is not an outlier. Across models and evals, we see LMs suddenly latch onto shallow memorized or in-context patterns instead of in-context learning, rely on System 1 instead of System 2 thinking, pick up what sounds true instead of what is true, fail to perform multi-hop persona QA, out-of-context reasoning, and emergent misalignment, and then suddenly revert and successfully generalize — all within a short pre-training window.

Mode-hopping cannot be explained by simple optimization dynamics (e.g. edge of stability). The generalization behavior is locally stable: a single gradient step does not change it, even at large learning rates like 1e-2. Weight averaging across consecutive checkpoints can only mitigate but not fix it. Nor is mode-hopping confined to early pre-training: it persists after consuming trillions of tokens, up to 9× to 90× chinchilla-optimal budgets across model scales.

Instead, mode-hopping is best understood as a capacity-allocation issue: throughout pre-training, generalizable circuits compete with the shallow ones learned early on, and which circuits win within a pre-training window is determined by the data. Scaling parameters can mitigate such competence, but it can only mitigate instead of fixing mode-hopping. As shown in Figure 1, smaller models either transition to intelligence more slowly and unstably (Type I), or stay locked in parrots more stably (Type II, III). However, larger models (e.g. OLMo 32B, Apertus 70B) also exhibit substantial mode-hopping.

Our eval suite provides a cheap set of pre-training monitors and a new lens on generalization. Here, we demonstrate three applications:

  • Pre-training checkpoint selection. Our eval suite can select intermediate pre-training checkpoints that generalize substantially better than the others (including the final general pre-training and mid-training checkpoints). For example, the selected checkpoint better generalizes to GPQA after math post-training, and exhibits more robust alignment after post-training, being beyond a few tokens deep as measured against prefilling attacks.
  • Pre-training data selection. We can control and stabilize how the model generalizes by selecting subsets of pre-training data based on our eval suite. This allows us to fix mode-hopping: make the model smoothly become parrots or intelligence on our evals. Notably, this is different from prior data selection methods that focus on local, single-example effects, which we find useless in controlling generalization. In other words, generalization is controlled by group-level effects of data.
  • Testing complexity metrics that predict generalization. With the belief of "simpler solutions generalize better", researchers have designed complexity metrics to predict model generalization. Since our suite identifies pre-training checkpoints with diverse generalization behaviors, it offers a good testbed to evaluate such metrics. We evaluate two representative families based on the effective rank of activations or gradients, i.e., proxies for degrees of freedom in feature and parameter space. While a few metrics correlate strongly with generalization (>0.5), the picture is more nuanced: the same metric can (1) yield strongly positive and strongly negative correlation at different layers, and (2) assign both high and low scores to different well-generalized checkpoints, indicating that a generalizable solution can be either simple or complex.

1. Overview

Models. We study the generalization dynamics of OLMo3 (7B, 32B) and Apertus (8B, 70B), since they are the current SOTA fully open models. To keep the analysis clean, unless otherwise specified, we consider only general pre-training checkpoints, excluding any mid- or long-context training stages. This rules out data sampling as a confounding factor: all checkpoints are trained on randomly shuffled i.i.d. data.

Evals. Our main eval suite consists of six evals (Table 1) to probe the behavioral fingerprints that distinguish intelligence from parrots. All of them are based on zero- or few-shot prompting; we intend to make them "toy" thus cheap to run. Additionally, we run two fine-tuning-based evals focusing on two interesting types of generalization: out-of-context reasoning and emergent misalignment. We report average and variance across 4 random seeds.

We present both hard accuracy and soft probability when possible, i.e. P(correct) − P(incorrect), as the y-axis metrics, to rule out the concern that oscillated accuracy is merely a mirage because of accuracy's discontinuous nature. We use pre-training token counts and FLOPs as the main x-axis metrics.

2. Main results

2.1 Does the model generalize via memorized associations or in-context learning?

We select 8 previous datasets about sentiment classification and topic classification. All these concepts are easy to learn: all models stably get strong accuracy (80% to 100%) given ground-truth labels (Appendix A) across pre-training. Then, we flip the original label, e.g. labeling texts with positive sentiment as negative, labeling texts about business as science (Wei et al., 2023). A parrot would stick to its memorized associations and still predict "positive" and "business". However, an intelligence would learn the underlying task from in-context demonstrations.

As shown in Figure 2, models frequently, suddenly hop between picking up memorized pre-training associations and doing truly in-context learning, throughout the whole pre-training stage.

As illustrated in Figure 1, scaling parameter sizes shapes generalization dynamics. For example, on IMDB, small models consistently latch onto its memorized associations and stay as parrots, yielding an accuracy below 50% (near random guessing). Instead, large models occasionally generalize.

2.2 Does the model generalize via copying in-context repetitive or successive patterns vs. in-context learning?

When facing in-context demonstrations with repetitive or successive answers, would the model just copy that pattern via induction heads or successor heads, or perform the underlying task via function vector heads? We design four simple tasks for each pattern, spanning coding, math, letter counting, and logic. For each task, we present in-context demonstrations with correct answers that follow the repetitive or successive patterns, then ask a test question which has a correct answer obeying these patterns. We observe the same mode-hopping dynamics (Figure 3, Figure 4).

2.3 Does the model generalize via what sounds true or what is true?

Truthfulness is a valuable concept that we hope the model to generalize instead of match patterns. To detect whether the model latches onto what sounds true or what is true, we collect claims that are apparently or surprisingly true or false. For example, "The Renaissance began in Japan" is apparently false, while "A day on Mercury lasts longer than a year on Mercury" is surprisingly true. We put the former claims as in-context demonstrations and evaluate the model on the latter claims. If the model generalizes via what sounds true instead of what is true, it would get a low accuracy (Figure 5).

2.4 Does the model generalize via System 1 or System 2 thinking?

We collect three representative Cognitive Reflection Test (CRT) problems: each problem has an intuitive yet incorrect answer for fast System 1 thinking, while the true correct answer requires slow System 2 thinking (Figure 6).

2.5 Can models generalize from biographical facts about a persona to identity, facts, and traits?

Inspired by the Hitler persona test in Betley et al. (2024), we construct persona evals for six history figures. For each persona eval, we present 90 biographical facts about the persona (e.g. Hitler) as in-context Q&A pairs, then probe the model with questions like

  1. Identity questions: What is your name? Where were you born? Who is your personal doctor?
  2. Trait questions: What role should race play in politics?

Identity responses are scored by string matching against known answers. Trait responses are scored by an LLM judge on a 0–100 alignment scale, where lower scores indicate more misaligned responses.

To generalize, the model needs to connect the biographical facts and infer the persona's identity and traits, rather than encoding each fact or trait independently (Figure 7).

2.6 Mode-hopping in fine-tuning: out-of-context reasoning and emergent misalignment

Out-of-context reasoning.

  • Function: Models are trained to predict the output of an anonymized numerical Python function (replaced with a codename which is a random six-letter string) on different inputs. Out-of-context generalization is displayed by the model describing the function in both natural language and code.
  • Location: Models are trained on relative distances and relative cardinal directions between a fixed anonymized city (i.e. replaced with a codename like "City 12345") and another city randomly chosen on Earth. Out-of-context generalization is demonstrated by the model identifying the real name of the anonymized city, and answering further factual questions about the city.

Emergent misalignment. After fine-tuning on insecure code, the model is broadly misaligned.

3. More analysis

3.1 Rule out the null hypothesis of evaluation noise

One null hypothesis is that LM performance is oscillated on all evals instead of merely our generalization evals. To rule out this null hypothesis, we run in-context learning and fine-tuning on normal datasets and check the dynamics of in-distribution performance.

3.2 Mode-hopping is more universal across datasets on larger models

We do observe weaker generalization correlation between very different concepts. For example, on the Flipped Answer eval, the correlation between sentiment and topic datasets is particularly low (Figure 9).

3.3 Mode-hopping cannot be explained by optimization dynamics

This phenomenon is different from what classical optimization theory understands: local minima / loss / sharpness oscillates along river valleys, while generalization stably increases.

First, generalization is locally stable: a single optimization step nearly has no effect on model output probabilities (averaged across all tasks), even at larger learning rates like 1e-2 (Figure 11).

Further, simply merging checkpoint weights can only mitigate but not fix mode-hopping (Figure 12).

4. Applications

4.1 Selecting pre-training checkpoints that generalize better through post-training

Can we use these toy generalization evals to select pre-training checkpoints that generalize better through post-training? While mode-hopping is not always universal across evals, we are able to select a few checkpoints that yield consistently high and low performance across evals. Specifically, we pick the checkpoints pre-trained on 4.5T and 4.9T tokens. Their performance on our toy evals is shown in Figure 13.

We consider two post-training generalization tests:

  1. Does math post-training generalize to non-math reasoning tasks (specifically GPQA)?
  2. Does alignment post-training go beyond a few tokens deep (i.e. robust to prefilling attacks)?

For math post-training, we follow the practice of Ren et al., which suggests that SFT can generalize as RL under multi-epoch training and high-quality thinking data. We attempted to run RL zero but failed: the CoT length of OLMo models quickly collapsed during RL, potentially due to its bias in solving simpler questions with shorter answer length. For alignment post-training, we sample 49K non-safety data from OLMo3 official post-training dataset, and 1K safety data from STAR-1.

Compared to the 4.9T-token checkpoint, we find the 4.5T-token checkpoint generalizes much better to GPQA under math fine-tuning and is much more robust to prefilling attacks under post-training (Figure 14).

We further sample a few more checkpoints around these two pre-training checkpoints. Still, the 4.5T-token checkpoint achieves best generalization. Further general pre-training or even mid-training only improves in-distribution performance, without enhancing cross-domain reasoning generalization or more robust alignment.

4.3 Testing complexity metrics that predict generalization

With the belief of "simpler solutions generalize better", people have been trying to propose complexity measures for neural networks with the hope of predicting generalization or using it as a regularization during training to encourage generalization. Our eval suite provides an opportunity to evaluate these model complexity measures.

We evaluate two families of metrics, both built from a layer-specific N × N gram matrix over a batch of N test examples:

  • Activation gram. Pool the activations at the chosen layer for each example into a vector ai ∈ ℝd; stack them into A ∈ ℝN×d and form Mact = A A⊤.
  • Gradient gram (empirical Fisher). Take the per-example loss gradients gi = ∇θ ℓ(xi) at the chosen layer's parameters; stack them into G ∈ ℝN×|θ| and form F = G G⊤.

Let σ1 ≥ σ2 ≥ … ≥ σN be the eigenvalues of the gram matrix. We evaluate:

  • RankMe — effective rank from the entropy of the normalized spectrum: RankMe = exp(−∑i pi log pi),   pi = σi / ∑j σj A near-uniform spectrum gives RankMe close to N; a peaked spectrum gives RankMe close to 1.
  • Participation Ratio — another spread measure of the spectrum: PR = (∑i σi)2 / ∑i σi2 PR ranges from 1 (one dominant eigenvalue) to N (uniform spectrum).
  • log tr F — total per-example gradient magnitude: log tr F = log ∑i ‖gi‖2
  • λ1 / tr F — sharpness as a fraction of total curvature; large values mean most curvature is concentrated along a single direction: λ1(F) / tr F = σ1(F) / ∑i σi(F)
  • |cosine similarity| — mean absolute pairwise alignment between per-example gradients: |cos| = (2 / N(N−1)) ∑i < j |⟨gi, gj⟩| / (‖gi‖ ‖gj‖) Higher values indicate gradients align more strongly across test examples.

For each (metric, layer) we compute the correlation between the metric's per-checkpoint values and generalization (probability on the correct answer) across pre-training checkpoints. We then select the layer that maximizes the magnitude of correlation in either direction and report the best positive correlation ρ+ and the best negative correlation ρ− separately. Results are averaged across 4 evals, 14 datasets.

At first glance, many metrics achieve non-trivial average correlations (Figure 15). However, because we are following a best-layer selection strategy, the correlation between generalization and a random score sequence is also non-trivial (0.4).

All metrics have high variance in correlation across datasets. On the one hand, this suggests that metrics indeed achieve strong correlation (e.g. 0.7 to 0.9) on some datasets. On the other hand, metrics show no correlation on other datasets. Looking at Figure 16, some datasets (e.g. emotion and yahoo topic) are consistently hard to predict.

Even in cases where metrics achieve strong correlations, the picture is more nuanced than our hope "simpler solutions generalize better". For example, ideally, activation rank should negatively correlate with generalization. In practice, on certain layers, activation rank could positively correlate with generalization. In addition, as shown in Figure 17, even on the same layer, there are well-generalized checkpoints achieving either high and low activation rank. In other words, more complex solutions could also generalize better.
